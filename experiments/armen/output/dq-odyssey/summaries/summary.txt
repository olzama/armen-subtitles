Full Analysis of Literary and Intertextual References

Overall Theme:
The text is a philosophically rich literary lecture contrasting The Odyssey by Homer and Don Quixote by Cervantes. It questions the validity of grouping these works together, arguing instead that they represent antithetical principles of movement, purpose, and worldview. It uses literary comparison as a tool for deeper reflection on cultural, psychological, and philosophical dimensions of storytelling and heroism.

Direct and Indirect References (Exhaustive List)

Primary Literary Works Discussed:
1. Homer – The Odyssey
   - Central reference: the archetypal journey of return (nostos).
   - Ulysses (Odysseus) as a clever, prudent hero seeking home and restoration.

2. Cervantes – Don Quixote
   - The anti-Odyssey: a journey outward, away from home (kóleos), not of return.
   - A quest motivated by ideals rather than practical goals.

3. Tolkien – The Hobbit
   - Bilbo Baggins is mentioned as a counterexample. His journey is a “there and back again,” more similar to The Odyssey than to Don Quixote.

Thematic and Philosophical Concepts:
- Nostos (νόστος): Greek concept of homecoming. Applied to Odysseus.
- Kóleos (κοῖλος?): Used here metaphorically for the centrifugal impulse of Don Quixote's journey.
- Journey as metaphor: contrasting centripetal (The Odyssey) vs. centrifugal (Don Quixote) paths.
- Identity and agency: heroes who “refuse the status quo,” but with opposite motivations and trajectories.

Comparative Literary Figures (Illustrative Analogies):
4. Joseph Brodsky vs. Yevgeny Yevtushenko
   - Used as a jarring comparison, meant to provoke. Highlights the inappropriateness of superficial pairings.

5. Vladimir Nabokov vs. Fyodor Dostoevsky
   - Another provocative pairing. Emphasizes the stylistic and philosophical chasm between writers often grouped by nationality alone.

Other Possible Allusions & Metaphorical Motifs:
6. “Two great wanderers of European literature”
   - Broader category to which Don Quixote and Odysseus supposedly belong.

7. “Costume similarity vs. substance difference”
   - Strong metaphor indicating that visual or surface similarities obscure deep existential and moral contrasts.

8. “All these coincidences are no more than similarities of costume”
   - Possibly an indirect nod to semiotic or structuralist readings.

9. “Прозваны хитроумными” / “ingenious”
   - Noted as a translation artifact: in Spanish, Don Quixote is called el ingenioso hidalgo, while Odysseus is prudent or wise.

Noted Citations or Poetry:
- No direct citation of poetry is found, but some phrases are elevated and rhythmic, and the mention of Greek terms like nostos and kóleos borders on scholarly poetic use.

Tone Nuances and Rhetorical Strategy:
- Tone: Erudite, ironic, provocative, emotionally engaged.
- Frequent use of irony and hyperbole: comparing literary figures in exaggerated ways to stress difference.
- Philosophical attitude: speaker treats literature as a reflection of human orientation in the world—toward home or the unknown.
