At night, somewhere around one o'clock, in a small provincial town with no specific name, a young man of about 27 or so enters a solitary wooden annex in the courtyard to visit an engineer-builder. Sitting on a sofa with a lattice back and hard leather cushions,
he almost immediately notices a plate with an untouched boiled chicken on the window.
My name is Armen Zakharian, and this is the literary channel Armen and Fyodor.
Today we're launching a new feature called "Episode".
In it, we will carefully examine one excerpt from the pages of world literature each time and analyze why this episode is special and how what we call art works in it.
And for this first experiment, we have chosen one of the most eerie, yet interesting scenes from Fyodor Dostoevsky's novel "Demons".
It's an episode from almost the very end of the book, from its concluding part: where Verkhovensky comes at night to a house where Kirillov is and sees a boiled chicken with rice on the window.
And although there's a loaded pistol right next to the chicken, it's not the gun, but the chicken that plays the role of Chekhov's gun in this scene, about to go off.
But to understand who these two are and how they ended up here in the same room at night in this outbuilding, we first need to figure out who they are.
Interestingly, even at the very beginning of the book, just introducing us to the characters, Dostoevsky is already laying the groundwork for their eerie nocturnal meeting.
Kirillov is a young man of about 27.
Verkhovensky mirrors him almost exactly—a young man of about 27 or so.
This intentional similarity, of course, only highlights how deep their differences are.
Kirillov is a vivid representative of Dostoevsky's famous line of fanatic heroes: heroes who become overly passionate about some idea, start serving this idea, living in the name of this idea.
But Kirillov has a different plan: he intends to die for his idea.
Kirillov is obsessed with the idea of the "man-God".
He arrives at it through a problem that the literature of this period in the second half of the 19th century often tried to solve: how to live without God.
Suppose God no longer exists. Suppose we no longer believe in Him; we have, as Verkhovensky might put it, unbaptized Him.
But the need to fill the void in His place remains.
And while another Dostoevsky hero in the same "Demons" says: "If there is no God, then what kind of captain can I be after that?"
—Kirillov says: "If there is no God, then I can take His place."
Remember, Grebenshchikov sang: "You feel the draft from this vacant spot," 
Kirillov feels that very draft and formulates a terrifying, insane thought that he then falls in love with.
If a person, he says, decides to declare by his own will, not out of anger or fear or spite but only because he has the right, leaves life, he argues, this step will rebirth all humanity and he himself will become a man-God.
But they tell him there have already been millions of suicides.
"But all not for this, all with fear and not for that,"—Kirillov argues back.
And this crazy approach of his remarkably resembles an episode from "Don Quixote".
Remember, when the daring hidalgo tells Sancho Panza that he plans to imitate the famous knights of the past and go mad.
The pragmatic Sancho then asks: "But what is the reason for you to go mad, señor? What is the reason?"—
"That's the point," Don Quixote replies. "That's the unusual part of the enterprise I have in mind: those wandering knights who went mad for some reason were not asking for reward or gratitude. The whole trick is to go crazy without any reason."
And as we'll soon see, these correspondences in the words of Kirillov and Quixote are far from coincidental.
So, to realize his insane idea, Kirillov has a loaded revolver next to the chicken on the window.
You might ask: but what is he waiting for then; why not carry out his intention since he says he needs no reason for it?
This is where his night visitor comes into the picture, revolutionary and nihilist Pyotr Stepanovich Verkhovensky.
This character is entirely different—a remarkable hero, simultaneously funny and frightening, comical and parodically terrifying, talkative and theatrical.
But at the same time, he's eerie, because he's ready for any evil.
Verkhovensky in some respects resembles Mephistopheles, only Mephistopheles had a better sense of humor. And due to his life experience and age, he was more modest.
However, they primarily share one thing: 
Verkhovensky is a highly practical man.
He thinks that the end justifies any means.
And when he comes across Kirillov's lovely project of ideological suicide, Verkhovensky immediately figures out how he can use it for the benefit of their cause.
No, not for the rebirth of humanity—he doesn't believe in that even slightly—but for his devilish deed.
He decides to commit a political murder and since Kirillov doesn't care when he dies, he asks him to write a confession to this murder before his death.
In other words, to use Kirillov's death as an alibi for his crime.
So, this is the setup at the time of their nocturnal meeting: a conflict of the spiritual (albeit sick, but Don Quixote was mad too) and the material; a conflict of the sublime and the base, the tragic and the pragmatic.
But how to show all this through a single image?
With the help of the chicken.
The episode, as is often the case with Dostoevsky, unfolds rapidly.
Verkhovensky has just entered, has just confirmed: like, "our intentions remain? I hope we stick to the suicide plan, we'll sign the paper?" and almost without pause goes straight there! Yes, I notice something there on the window, on the plate.
He approaches the window.
Wow, a boiled chicken with rice, but why is it still untouched?
Well, then we must be in such a state of mind that even the chicken...
Kirilov interrupts him, "And it's none of your business, be quiet!
Of course, and yet it's all the same.
But for me, it's not the same now, imagine, I almost haven't eaten, and so if now the chicken, as I suppose, is no longer needed..."
Note how Kirillov formulates his response: "Eat it if you can."
Verkhovensky can, oh, he can very well; he can for himself and that guy.
"Well, thank you," he says, and then, "and some tea."
And follows something even more terrifying, a cherry on the cake of this scene.
Verkhovensky instantly settles at the table on the other end of the sofa and throws himself upon the meal with extraordinary greed, at the same time constantly watching his victim.
Kirillov stares at him with angry aversion, immobile, as if unable to tear himself away.
Verkhovensky here is the triumph of the flesh, he eats greedily, voraciously, with zest.
After all, the person sitting a meter from him is about to shoot himself, but since the chicken is now vacant.
Notice, in this single paragraph, Verkhovensky takes absolutely everything from Kirillov: his freedom, his last will, his last meal, and in the end, his life.
The chicken is, of course, just a metaphor because inherently, Verkhovensky is a cannibal, people for him are expendable material, a material that feeds his pathetic ambitions and plans.
Does the happiness of humanity stand up to a child's tear?—asks Ivan Karamazov.
“And is the little child served with a side dish or just like that? asks Pyotr.
And Kirillov, this strange Kirillov, yes, with his insane idea, but still with no evil heart, can't tear himself away, he watches with horror as Verkhovensky devours his chicken.
Because it's not just the chicken he's devouring, but him, Kirillov, he's about to eat.
Verkhovensky: "Barabek devoured 40 people, and a cow, and a bull, and a crooked butcher, and a cart and a yoke, and a broom, and a poker, devoured the chicken with a side dish and ended this world.”
And this whole tangle of contradictions, fears, and fates Dostoevsky shows through one dining scene, through one plate of cold chicken with rice.
The eloquent horror of this gastronomic scene was very accurately captured in the legendary production of "Demons" by the Saint Petersburg's Small Drama Theater in Lev Dodin’s six-hour play that ran on stage for almost 30 years.
Those lucky enough to have attended this performance may surely remember the scene with Verkhovensky's chicken.
Seeing it live once is difficult to forget.
